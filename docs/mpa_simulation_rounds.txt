COMPARATIVE PUBLIC ADMINISTRATION SIMULATION DESIGN DOCUMENT
Round 1: Constituting the Comparative Governance Assembly
Detailed Design Specification — Mechanics, Content, Gating Logic, Persistent Variables, and Consequence Hooks for Development Handoff


1. Overview
Learners (a group of 10 students) constitute the Comparative Governance Assembly, a fictional multinational legislative body established to address a growing public administration crisis affecting both developed and developing countries.
Rather than immediately debating reforms, students first establish the Assembly, define the governance crisis, assign institutional responsibilities, and determine the Assembly’s operating principles. Every decision made in this round becomes part of the Assembly Charter and influences the remaining simulation rounds.
Learning Focus
* Foundations of Comparative Public Administration
* Comparative governance systems
* Legislative organisation
* Administrative context analysis
* Strategic reform planning
Group Size
10 students (one role each, no duplicates)
Starting Governance Resources
The Assembly begins with the following governance resources rather than financial resources.
* Administrative Capacity — 100
* Public Trust — 100
* Political Capital — 100
* Stakeholder Support — 100
* Reform Momentum — 100
These variables fluctuate throughout subsequent rounds depending on legislative decisions.

2. Assembly Roles[a][b]
Each group represents the legislature of its assigned country. Since legislative structures differ across countries, students must first identify the principal officers of the legislature they are representing before assigning roles.
This encourages students to research the institutional structure of their assigned legislature and ensures that the simulation accurately reflects real-world legislative practice.
Step 0A – Select Your Legislature
At the start of the simulation, the group selects (or confirms) its assigned country from the course list.
Once selected, the simulation automatically loads the legislative information for that country’s parliament or assembly, including:
* Name of the legislature
* Legislative structure (unicameral or bicameral)
* Chamber(s)
* Principal legislative offices
For example:

The simulation can easily be extended to all countries listed in the course.


Step 0B – Assign Legislative Roles
Each student selects one legislative office from the list generated for the selected country’s legislature.
The simulation displays only the offices applicable to that legislature.
Groups may also add additional country-specific offices where necessary to accurately reflect their assigned legislature.
Role Assignment Rules
* Every student must be assigned one legislative office.
* No principal office may be assigned to more than one student unless that legislature permits multiple officeholders.
* Progress is blocked until all required offices have been assigned.


Dynamic Role Authority
Throughout the simulation, all students participate in discussions and decision-making.
However, only the student occupying the appropriate legislative office may approve actions that fall within that office’s constitutional responsibility.
Because legislative offices vary by country, approval authority is dynamically mapped to the selected legislature rather than being fixed across all groups.
For example:
* The Presiding Officer (Speaker, Senate President, President of Parliament, etc.) opens proceedings, recognises speakers, and oversees voting.
* The Clerk records proceedings, reads motions, and certifies legislative documents.
* The Majority Leader presents government business and legislative proposals.
* The Minority Leader leads scrutiny and proposes amendments.
* Committee Chairs present committee reports and recommendations.
* Other legislative officers perform responsibilities consistent with their country’s parliamentary procedures.

3. Core Mechanic: Role-Gated Confirmation
Every student contributes to every discussion.
However, each Assembly decision can only be officially adopted by the officer responsible for that area.
This mirrors real legislative accountability.
Rules
* Every student debates every decision.
* Only the designated Assembly officer may press “Confirm Resolution.”
* Other members can comment but cannot finalise the decision.
* The interface clearly displays which officer owns each approval.
* The confirmation button remains disabled for all other members.
This approval mechanic becomes a recurring interaction throughout the simulation.
